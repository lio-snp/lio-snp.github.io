## Research Origin

The [previous note](/en/blog/td3-intrinsic-action-freeze/) focused on a toy mechanism question: why an intrinsic signal can move value estimates without changing actions.

This new note moves one step closer to the LESR search loop itself: why do we keep finding candidates whose `intrinsic_mean` is clearly non-zero while `G3` is almost indistinguishable from `G1`?

Everything below is grounded in the current experiment workspace as of **March 9, 2026**.

## 1. Symptom: non-zero intrinsic, zero policy delta

I will use the usual three-group shorthand:

- `G0`: baseline
- `G1`: revise state only
- `G3`: revise state plus intrinsic reward

Across 8 smoke runs collected on March 9, 2026 (`step52` and `step53`, `selected_4` and `composite`, four algorithms each), `30/32` algorithm cells satisfy both of the following:

1. `G1` and `G3` have exactly the same `Sharpe / CR / MDD / AV`;
2. `G3` still shows a clearly non-zero `intrinsic_mean`.

## 2. A first-principles test: what counts as policy-relevant intrinsic?

Write the critic-side total value as

$$
Q_{tot}(s,a)=Q_{env}(s,a)+wB(s)+w\Delta(s,a).
$$

Here `B(s)` is a baseline-like state-only term, while `\Delta(s,a)` is the action-dependent part that can change action ranking. The policy only cares about

$$
a^\star(s)=\arg\max_a Q_{tot}(s,a).
$$

So `intrinsic_reward(s)\neq 0` only tells us that the level of $Q_{tot}$ moved. To actually change the chosen action, we need

$$
w\cdot\bigl(\Delta(s,a_1)-\Delta(s,a_2)\bigr)
$$

to exceed the environment's own action gap.

In short,

$$
\text{non-zero intrinsic} \not\Rightarrow \text{policy-relevant intrinsic}.
$$

This is continuous with the previous toy note. That note explained why value can move while action stays fixed. This one asks the next question: **why should the searcher treat such a candidate as useful at all?**

## 3. How this rewrites LESR candidate scoring

If candidate search only rewards short-budget score gains, it will over-select two kinds of false positives:

- candidates that mostly shift value levels without changing action ranking;
- candidates whose intrinsic channel is numerically active but almost useless under raw-policy control.

That is why the current LESR scorer no longer looks only at `Sharpe + CR`. It is evolving toward a composite objective of the form

$$
S(c)=\lambda_{perf}\Delta_{G3-G0}(c)+\lambda_{lip}\frac{1}{1+L(c)}+\lambda_{beh}B(c)+\lambda_{probe}P(c).
$$

where

- $\Delta_{G3-G0}$ is performance delta to baseline;
- $L(c)$ is an intrinsic Lipschitz proxy to penalize brittle candidates;
- $B(c)$ is action-behavior robustness, built from near-bound ratio, entropy, and action diversity;
- $P(c)$ is an intrinsic probe under raw-policy control, directly asking whether the intrinsic path still helps when the policy side is held fixed.

The point is not that the score became more complicated. The point is that the search target now explicitly separates **numeric activity** from **policy relevance**.

The March 9 runs also changed candidate supply on the prompt side by intentionally mixing `intrinsic_first`, `balanced`, and `state_first` designs. The goal was no longer "produce more intrinsic candidates", but "produce more intrinsic candidates that remain useful under raw-policy control."

The most important sentence in this table is simple: **setup can flip the sign of the overall gain, while "non-zero intrinsic yet zero policy delta" remains a separate failure mode.**

Best-family selection also does not converge to one global winner. In `selected_4`, A2C and TD3 leaned toward `risk_shield` while PPO and SAC leaned toward `trend_follow`. In `composite`, PPO shifted to `risk_shield` and SAC shifted to `mean_revert`. That makes the problem look algorithm-conditional rather than a single global-best candidate problem.

## 5. What research questions does this point to now?

Given the current evidence, I would frame the next stage as three research questions instead of claiming universal LESR gains:

1. **How should we measure candidate policy relevance directly?**  
   Possible directions include action-ranking flip rate, raw-policy probe delta, or more explicit control-sensitive counterfactuals.

2. **Why does `composite` convert LESR signals into gains more easily than `selected_4`?**  
   This looks more like a state-separability and cross-sectional variation question than a simple intrinsic magnitude question.

3. **Why do different algorithms prefer different candidate families?**  
   If stable policy-relevant selection requires per-algorithm branches, then cross-algorithm distillation becomes a method question of its own.

## Limitations

- This is smoke evidence, not a confirmatory benchmark.
- The key runs are all short-budget configurations, typically `n_small=8` and `n_full=30`.
- These results support the existence of a sharper failure mode and the need to change the search target. They do not support a claim that the method is already fully solved.

## Conclusion

If I phrase the LESR objective more precisely, it is not "any non-zero intrinsic should create gains." It is:

> LESR only has a chance to generate stable positive gains when state rewriting and the intrinsic term jointly change the **control-relevant ordering** of actions.

So the immediate engineering task is not "make intrinsic larger." It is to

- make intrinsic more **policy-relevant**,
- test that relevance **during candidate selection**,
- and only then ask in which regimes, algorithms, and universe setups that relevance turns into stable returns.
